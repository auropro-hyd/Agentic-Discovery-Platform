Enforced second approval above the thresholdHITL Workflow

Build the policy's second-approval requirement directly into the order approval step so that no order above EUR 50,000 can be committed until a second approver has signed off.

Before

1. Raise purchase order
Buyer
Buyer raises an order; the largest single order seen was EUR 120,000.
2. Single approval and commit
Approver
Order is committed after a single approval, even above the threshold. 7 orders worth EUR 557,000 followed this path.

After

1. Raise purchase order
Buyer
Buyer raises an order and the committed amount is checked against the EUR 50,000 threshold.
2. First approval
Approver
First approver reviews and approves the order.
3. Mandatory second approval above threshold
Second approver
Orders above the threshold are routed to a second approver and cannot be committed until that approval is given.

Business impact. Enforcing the second approval at the point of commitment brings the company's largest orders back under control. It directly addresses the EUR 557,000 of spend, across 7 orders and 91.9% of total value, that was committed on a single approval. EUR 557,000 of committed spend   7 orders   91.9% of total value

How it's delivered. Embed the approval threshold and second-approver routing into the order approval step, with a hard stop preventing commitment until the second sign-off is recorded.

Connects: Purchase order records, Approval routing

Success looks like:

  • No orders above the threshold committed on a single approval
  • All orders above EUR 50,000 carry a recorded second approval

Dependencies: Independent — can start immediately. Prerequisite for the AI Credit Decisioning step.

Risks:

  • Approver availability could slow commitments if no delegation is in place

Where this comes from: your Procurement Policy and your Purchase Order Export

Order-first control before supplier commitmentAutomation Pipeline

Require that a valid purchase order exists before any commitment can be made to a supplier, closing the path that lets spend happen before an order is raised.

Before

1. Commit to supplier
Buyer
Buyer commits to a supplier before an order exists. 3 orders worth EUR 216,000, or 25.0% of orders, followed this path, all on a single approval.
2. Document order afterwards
Buyer
An order is recorded after the commitment has already been made.

After

1. Raise and validate order
Buyer
A purchase order is raised and validated before any supplier commitment can proceed.
2. Commit only against an existing order
Buyer
Supplier commitment is blocked unless a valid order already exists, removing the EUR 216,000 order-after-commit path.

Business impact. Requiring an order before commitment restores budget control and three-way matching. It addresses the EUR 216,000, across 3 orders and 25.0% of orders, committed before any order existed. Because all of this value also carries the single-approval issue, the EUR 216,000 also benefits from the enforced second approval. EUR 216,000   3 orders   25.0% of orders   EUR 216,000 also on single approval

How it's delivered. Add an order-exists validation as a precondition to supplier commitment, building on the enforced approval step so commitments cannot proceed without both a valid order and the required approvals.

Connects: Purchase order records, Supplier commitment step

Success looks like:

  • No supplier commitments made before a valid order exists
  • Three-way matching possible on all orders

Dependencies: Requires Customer Master Reconciliation first. Prerequisite for the AI Credit Decisioning step.

Risks:

  • Urgent purchases may need a controlled fast-track exception path

Where this comes from: your Procurement Policy and your Purchase Order Export

Named owner and monitoring for procurement controlsAI Agent

Assign an accountable owner for the approval and order-first controls, supported by ongoing monitoring that surfaces concentrated non-compliance such as the pattern seen in one buyer.

Before

1. No monitoring
Not assigned
No one is assigned to monitor adherence, so concentrated non-compliance of 2 orders worth EUR 149,000, or 24.6% of value, goes undetected.
2. No remediation
Not assigned
Repeat behaviour persists with no exception or remediation process.

After

1. Assign control owner
Control owner
A named owner is accountable for the approval and order-first controls and for granting exceptions.
2. Continuous monitoring and alerting
Control owner
Order activity is monitored continuously, surfacing concentrations such as the EUR 149,000 in one buyer for timely review.
3. Remediate and document exceptions
Control owner
Flagged cases are reviewed, remediated, and any exceptions documented.

Business impact. A named owner with continuous monitoring catches concentrated non-compliance before it persists. It targets the 2 orders worth EUR 149,000, or 24.6% of value, concentrated in one buyer, and provides the oversight layer that makes the enforced controls durable. 2 orders from one buyer   EUR 149,000   24.6% of value

How it's delivered. Define accountability for the controls in the policy, then stand up continuous monitoring that watches order activity against the enforced approval and order-first rules and alerts the owner to concentrations and exceptions.

Connects: Purchase order records, Monitoring and alerting

Success looks like:

  • Every control has a named accountable owner
  • Concentrated non-compliance surfaced and remediated within a defined window

Dependencies: Requires Customer Master Reconciliation first.

Risks:

  • Monitoring is only as effective as the enforced controls it watches

Where this comes from: your Procurement Policy and your Purchase Order Export
