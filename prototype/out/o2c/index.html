1. Current State Assessment

How Order-to-Cash runs today. A factual baseline of the process, the systems that support it, and how information is structured — stated as fact, no judgements.

1.1 Domain overview

This assessment covers the order-to-cash process for the European retail business — how customer orders are received, credit-checked, released, fulfilled, and how related disputes are resolved. The process spans three operating layers: customer and credit master information held in two separate systems, order intake across electronic and manual channels, and the customer-service activity that catches and resolves failures. The largest retail accounts dominate both the order value and the credit limits that flow through this process.

Customer orders arrive through an electronic data interchange channel and through manual channels (such as email and direct entry). Each order is checked against a credit limit held for that customer before it is released for fulfilment. Credit limits and payment terms are maintained in a customer-relationship system and in the core enterprise resource planning system. When an order fails to flow through — most commonly an electronic order that needs manual re-entry — it is picked up by customer service and worked through to resolution. Two-thirds of order volume runs through the electronic channel.

1.3 Systems & sources

Each system that supports this process — what it is, how it is used, who owns it, and the constraints observed.

Core enterprise resource planning system

The operational backbone for the customer master, order processing, and credit control. The credit policy names it as the single system of record for credit limits and payment terms.

How it's used. Orders are processed and released against the credit limits it holds; master-data and credit teams maintain customer records here.

Ownership & access. Master-data and credit teams maintain the records; order management and fulfilment teams operate against it day to day.

Observed constraints. Its stored credit limits differ from those held in the customer-relationship system for 267 of 318 matched customers, and payment terms differ for 228 customers.

Customer-relationship system

A parallel commercial view of customer credit limits and payment terms used alongside the system of record.

How it's used. Referenced during commercial and credit interactions; in practice some credit decisions and order releases are made against the limits it holds.

Ownership & access. Commercial and credit teams.

Observed constraints. It is not the policy's system of record, yet holds limits that exceed the system of record by up to 600000 EUR for a single account and 30675000 EUR in aggregate.

Electronic data interchange channel

The primary order-intake route, carrying 67.3 percent of orders by count and 66.8 percent of order value.

How it's used. Retail customers transmit orders electronically; a portion require manual re-entry before they can proceed.

Ownership & access. Not assigned — the channel is not covered by the order management procedure or the responsibility matrix.

Observed constraints. Electronic orders show 1196 unfulfilled orders worth 12362493.74 EUR, the largest single unfulfilled bucket, and a markedly higher non-fulfilment rate than manual orders.

Customer service records and informal working notes

The record of escalations and how failed orders are resolved.

How it's used. Customer service logs escalations and works electronic-order disputes through informal notes rather than a documented procedure.

Ownership & access. Customer service team.

Observed constraints. The most frequent escalation root cause — an electronic order needing manual intervention — accounts for 34 escalations, 23.9 percent of all escalations, and has no documented owner.

1.5 Process inventory

  • Customer and credit master set-up — Establish each retail customer with a credit limit and payment terms across the operating systems.
  • Order intake — Receive customer orders through electronic and manual channels and prepare them for release.
  • Credit check and release — Confirm each order sits within the customer's approved credit before it proceeds to fulfilment.
  • Fulfilment — Deliver released orders to the customer.
  • Dispute and escalation resolution — Catch and resolve orders that fail to process, fulfil, or arrive.

1.8 Handoff catalogue

  • Customer and credit master set-up → Credit check and order release (Credit limit read from one of two systems)
  • Electronic order intake → Credit check and order release (Electronic order, sometimes requiring manual re-entry)
  • Manual order intake → Credit check and order release (Keyed order following the order management procedure)
  • Credit check and order release → Fulfilment (Released order)
  • Fulfilment → Dispute and escalation handling (Unfulfilled or unreceived order raised as an escalation)
